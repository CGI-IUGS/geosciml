Ah, the siliciclastic problem. The red herring is of course carbonate-clastic rocks--sandstones composed of clasts composed of calcite. These are limestones and sandstones. The use of clastic is closely related to the discussion about how to represent composition of rocks. If you follow the logic I prefer--a rock is an aggregation of particles that have some genesis, particle geometry, and composition (which may be any Earth Material, including voids if you want to describe pore space), then
--- a clastic rock is composed (predominantly) of particles that have epiclastic genesis, composition unspecified,
--- a carbonate rock is composed (predominantly) of particles that are not epiclastic, and are composed of a carbonate mineral

so the logic is
Sed Rock -> epiclastic  (genetic) - > sandstone (grainsize)
Sed Rock -> not epiclastic  (genetic) -> Carbonate (compositional) -> Carbonate grainstone (grainsize).

the implication is that the other composition categories (organic, non-clastic silica, Fe-Rich, Phosphatic...) are also composed of not-epiclastic particles.

Like all lithology vocabularies, it does not work with a single Parent column.  There are too many examples of lithology terms having more than one parent terms. eg, rhyolitic rock -> “fine grained igneous rock” or “acidic igneous rock”?  Suggest you add a 2nd or 3rd parent column - one for compositional parent, the other for genetic or grainsize parent and serialise the vocabulary with more than one parent relationship.

 

The Sedimentary Rock (and Sediment) parent-child hierarchy switches from genetic to grainsize to compositional - eg: Sed Rock -> Clastic Sed Rock (genetic) - > Mudstone (grainsize); but Sed Rock -> Carbonate Sed Rock (compositional) -> Carbonate Mudstone (grainsize).  Suggest that “siliciclastic sed rock” might be a better term than “clastic sed rock”, because its children (sandstone, mudstone) have implied silicate composition and then the immediate children of “Sed Rock” (siliclastic, silicic non-clastic, carbonate, organic, Fe-rich, phosphatic etc) are mainly differentiated on compositional grounds (with some genetic overtones).

 

ie:

Sed rock

  Siliclastic sed rock

     Conglomerate

     Sandstone

     Mudstone

  Carbonate sed rock

     Carbonate Mudstone

 

The “Sedimentary material” category, and all of the compositional subtypes of sedimentary material underneath it are not needed.  “Sediment” (for unconsolidated) and “Sedimentary Rock” (for consolidated) are all that is needed. 

            a. The occasions that a geologist would not be able to classify a sedimentary material as either “sedimentary rock” or “sediment” are insignificant.

            b. Having the “sedimentary material” subcategories as well as all the sediment and sedimentary rock subcategories makes for unnecessary duplicate and potentially confusing hierarchy on sediments and sedimentary rocks.

 

Surficial regolith deposits are an extremely important feature of the surface geology of Australia.   As such, the lack of regolith terms is limiting.

  -  The exclusion of “clay” as a material because it is “not common enough” is not acceptable for the Australian continent.  Australian regolith is full of it, and the term is used often.

  -  Residual materials are lacking in the vocabulary.  Need terms like “saprolith” or “saprolite” to cater for the bauxites, laterites etc.

  -  A single “duricrust” term to cater for ferricrete, calcrete, silcrete etc seems limiting and inconsistent with the much more detailed compositional breakup of other sedimentary rocks (iron, phosphatic, siliceous, carbonate, dolomitic).

  -  Need categories for other unconsolidated deposits like soil, loess, parna

 

Don’t like the generic Breccia category.  It adds yet another potential parent hierarchy scheme to the vocabulary (besides the existing genetic, compositional and grainsize hierarchies).  Suggest that breccias be handled under headings of Sedimentary Breccia (siliciclastic and carbonate), Igneous Breccia (pyroclastic and intrusive), Fault (+ impact?) Breccia, and Hydrothermal Breccia.

 

No category exists for coarser grained rocks in QAPF field 12, or any of the rocks in QAPF field 13 (eg, foid monzosyenite, nepheline monzodiorite, essexite)

 

Syenite and monzonite are in the vocabulary, but the deprecated list says to leave them out.  If monzogranite and syenogranite (subcategories of QAPF 3) are not included in the vocabulary, then syenite and monzonite (subcategories of QAPF 7 and 8) should not be included either.

 

There is inconsistent categorisation of metamorphic rocks.  There are some metamorphic rock names indicating very specific compositions or facies/grades (eg, quartzite, marble, amphibolite, eclogite, serpentinite).  But “schist” has to cover rocks of acid to ultrabasic composition (what about blue schist, greenschist, felsic schist etc).  And granofels has to cover rocks from medium grade granofels to high grade granulites. 

                                                                                                                                                                                          

No adequate category(s) exists for metasomatic/hydrothermal products - eg, calcsilicates, skarns, greisens, ores (especially sulphides).  “Composite genesis rock” is an impossibly broad classification to handle metasomatic rocks.

 

There are no terms for vein rocks - eg, quartz veins, calcite veins

 

The lumping of some grainsizes is not helpful at times.  Suggest using “medium grained” where appropriate - eg, for dolerites which are lumped in with fine grained rocks. Basalts are fine grained, dolerites are medium grained, gabbros are coarse grained.  Likewise, not happy with the exclusion of grainsize terms “silt” and “siltstone”.  “Silt” is a widely recognised sedimentary grainsize, and having to map silt and siltstone to mud and mudstone is not good.  (An equivalent would be to exclude “phyllite” from the well known “slate” to “schist” sequence.)

 

“Foid gabbroic rock” and “Foid dioritic rock” are unnecessarily fine splitting of a single QAPF field.  Merge them.

 

Suggest merging Foiditic rock and Foidolite.  The existing categories are splitting hairs for rare rocks.

 

“Tuff-breccia, agglomerate, or pyroclastic breccia” and “Ash tuff, lapillistone, and lapilli tuff” are not good names.  They are just concatenations of approximate synonyms.  Prefer “coarse grained pyroclastic rock” and “fine to medium grained pyroclastic rock”

 

Mudstone is described as “aphanitic”, therefore Carbonate mudstone should also be “aphanitic”, not “fine grained”.

 

Parent of “Sediment” is “unconsolidated material”, meaning that “Natural unconsolidated material” and “Anthropogenic unconsolidated material” have no subtypes.   Potential for user confusion is high with having these childless categories.
